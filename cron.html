Understanding Cron Syntax

A cron expression is a string composed of five fields separated by spaces. Each field represents a unit of time and defines when a scheduled task (cron job) should execute. Cron is the standard job scheduler in Unix-like operating systems and is widely used in server administration, DevOps, CI/CD pipelines, and application scheduling.

Special Characters

  • * (asterisk) -- matches any value. * * * * * runs every minute.
  • , (comma) -- specifies a list. 1,3,5 in the weekday field means Monday, Wednesday, and Friday.
  • - (hyphen) -- specifies a range. 9-17 in the hour field means every hour from 9 AM to 5 PM.
  • / (slash) -- specifies a step. */15 in the minute field means every 15 minutes.

Common Cron Patterns Explained

0 0 * * * -- At minute 0 of hour 0 (midnight) every day. This is the standard daily cron job. Use it for backups, log rotation, or cleanup tasks.

*/15 * * * * -- Every 15 minutes, every hour, every day. Good for polling external APIs or checking system health.

0 9 * * 1-5 -- At 9:00 AM, Monday through Friday. Perfect for workday tasks like sending morning reports or triggering CI/CD pipelines.

0 0 1 * * -- At midnight on the 1st of every month. Used for monthly reports, billing cycles, or archiving old data.

Tips for Writing Cron Expressions

  • Always test your cron expression before deploying. Use the visual builder above to verify the next run times.
  • Be aware of time zones. Cron typically runs in the server's local time zone unless configured otherwise.
  • Avoid scheduling too many tasks at the same time (e.g., minute 0 of every hour). Spread out your jobs to avoid resource contention.
  • Use */n syntax for regular intervals instead of listing every value.
  • Remember that 0 in the weekday field represents Sunday, and 6 represents Saturday.
  • If a cron job takes longer than the interval, consider adding a lock mechanism to prevent overlapping runs.

Where Are Cron Expressions Used?

Cron expressions are used across many platforms and tools beyond the traditional Unix crontab:

  • Linux/Unix crontab -- The original cron scheduler for system tasks.
  • GitHub Actions -- Schedule workflows using cron syntax in on.schedule.
  • AWS CloudWatch Events / EventBridge -- Trigger Lambda functions on a schedule.
  • Kubernetes CronJobs -- Schedule containerized tasks in your cluster.
  • Jenkins -- Trigger builds at regular intervals with cron-like scheduling.
  • CI/CD pipelines -- Scheduled builds, tests, and deployments.
  • Task schedulers -- Celery Beat (Python), node-cron (Node.js), Quartz (Java), and many more.
